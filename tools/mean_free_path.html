Mean free path calculator

The present tool evaluates the mean free path of one or more species in a gas mixture from the VSS collision parameters of each species, and reports the DSMC numerical bounds \(\Delta x \lesssim \lambda/3\) and \(\Delta t \lesssim \tau_c/3\) that the mesh and time-step must respect (see Kinetic foundations and the DSMC algorithm). The tool exposes both the pure-species table (one line per species) and the pair table (one line per active collision pair), so that the cross parameters \((d_{st}, \omega_{st})\) that govern the binary interactions can be edited independently — because the rigorous VSS mixture calculation treats the cross terms as distinct fitted parameters, not as means of the pure-species values.

A short derivation, the combining-rule convention used to initialise the pair table, and a discussion of the cases in which the combining rule is inadequate are given at the end of the page. The calculator runs entirely in the browser: no input is sent to any server.

Show the formula used

The mean free path of species s in a mixture is the reciprocal of the sum of its collision frequencies with every species t in the mixture:

λs = 1 / Σt   nt · π · dst2 · (Tref / T)ωst − 1/2 · (1 + ms/mt)1/2

The cross parameters dst and ωst are taken from the pair table (section 4). When the pair entry has not been overridden, they are initialised from the arithmetic-mean combining rule, dst = (ds+dt)/2 and ωst = (ωs+ωt)/2, which is not rigorous — the background section at the bottom of the page discusses when this approximation is adequate and when it is not. The reference temperature is Tref = 273 K, the per-species number density is nt = xt · p / (kB T), and the mean thermal speed is c̄s = √(8 kB T / π ms), which yields the mean collision time τc,s = λs / c̄s.

Note the asymmetry of the pair factor (1 + ms/mt)1/2: s and t are not interchangeable in this formula. The self-contribution (t = s) recovers the factor √2 correctly, since (1 + ms/ms)1/2 = √2.

Background

Derivation

Consider a single molecule of species \(s\) moving through a gas mixture. Its collision frequency with species \(t\) is the product of the target number density \(n_t\), the binary collisional cross section \(\sigma_{st}(g)\), and the mean relative speed \(\bar{g}_{st}\):

\[\nu_{st} = n_t\,\sigma_{st}(g)\,\bar{g}_{st}, \qquad \bar{g}_{st} = \sqrt{\frac{8\,k_B T}{\pi\,\mu_{st}}},\]

with \(\mu_{st} = m_s m_t / (m_s + m_t)\) the reduced mass. Summing over target species and dividing by the thermal speed of species \(s\) yields

\[\lambda_s = \frac{\bar{c}_s}{\displaystyle \sum_t n_t\,\sigma_{st}\,\bar{g}_{st}}, \qquad \bar{c}_s = \sqrt{\frac{8\,k_B T}{\pi\,m_s}}.\]

For a VSS cross section \(\sigma_{st}(g) = \pi\,d_{st}^{2}\,(T_{\mathrm{ref}}/T)^{\omega_{st} - 1/2} /\Gamma(5/2 - \omega_{st})\), the \(\Gamma\) factor and the thermal-speed ratio combine to give

\[\lambda_s = \frac{1} {\displaystyle\sum_{t} n_t\,\pi\,d_{st}^{2}\, \left(\tfrac{T_{\mathrm{ref}}}{T}\right)^{\omega_{st} - 1/2}\, \left(1 + \tfrac{m_s}{m_t}\right)^{1/2}},\]

which is the expression evaluated by the calculator. Note in particular:

  • The inner sum is a sum over the target species \(t\), not a weighted average. Each term contributes additively to the collision frequency.

  • The mass factor \((1 + m_s/m_t)^{1/2}\) is asymmetric in \((s, t)\): it is not the same as \((1 + m_t/m_s)^{1/2}\). The self-contribution \(t = s\) gives \(\sqrt{2}\), recovering the classic single-species result.

  • The pair parameters \(d_{st}\) and \(\omega_{st}\) are in general fitted independently for each binary pair, from viscosity, diffusion, and thermal-conductivity data. They are not derived from the pure-species values through any mean.

Combining rules — when they apply, when they do not

When independent pair fits are not available, a combining rule is used to generate \(d_{st}\) and \(\omega_{st}\) from the pure-species values \(d_s, d_t, \omega_s, \omega_t\). The calculator initialises the pair table through Bird’s arithmetic-mean rule [Bird1994m],

\[d_{st} = \tfrac{1}{2}\,(d_s + d_t), \qquad \omega_{st} = \tfrac{1}{2}\,(\omega_s + \omega_t),\]

which is analogous to the Lorentz-Berthelot rule used for Lennard-Jones mixtures and is also the default convention in most DSMC solvers. The arithmetic-mean rule is adequate when:

  • the species involved are chemically similar (same family, similar polarisability);

  • the pure-species \(\omega_s\) are close to each other (within \(\pm 0.1\));

  • quantitative accuracy at the \(\sim\!10\%\) level on transport coefficients is sufficient.

It becomes unreliable when:

  • the colliding species differ greatly in mass or size (e.g. H2 – CO2);

  • one of the species is polar and the other is not, so that a weakly attractive interaction becomes dominant;

  • a chemically reactive pair (e.g. O + NO) is involved, whose cross sections are better fitted from QCT calculations than from transport data.

For production DSMC work, the pair parameters should therefore be obtained from:

  • experimental binary diffusion or binary viscosity data (fit \(d_{st}, \omega_{st}\) at the pair level);

  • classical-trajectory calculations on the relevant potential-energy surface;

  • the pair tables compiled in Koura and Matsumoto [Koura1991m] or Hirschfelder et al. [HCB1954m].

Any of these may be entered directly in the pair table (section 4 of the calculator). Overridden entries are highlighted and take precedence over the combining rule; they persist when the pure-species values are edited, and are cleared only by the Reset pair table to combining rule button.

DSMC numerical constraints

The DSMC convergence conditions of Kinetic foundations and the DSMC algorithm require that the spatial and temporal resolution respect the local kinetic scales:

\[\Delta x \lesssim \frac{\lambda}{3}, \qquad \Delta t \lesssim \frac{\tau_c}{3},\]

with \(\tau_c = \lambda / \bar{c}\) the mean collision time. Violating either bound introduces a bias in the collision statistics that is difficult to diagnose a posteriori. In a multi-species mixture, the binding constraint is the smallest \(\lambda_s\) across all species present — typically the heaviest, slowest species at the highest local pressure. The calculator identifies this binding species and reports the corresponding \(\Delta x\) and \(\Delta t\) bounds.

On the sub-cell machinery

When the flow spans several decades in pressure or temperature, a fixed mesh cannot satisfy \(\Delta x < \lambda/3\) everywhere simultaneously. The solver’s sub-cell adaptation layer (dsmcDynamicAdapter — sub-cell and weight adaptation) is the operational fix: each cell is logically subdivided, on the fly, into a number of sub-cells per direction that keeps the sub-cell size below the local mean free path. The calculator’s \(\Delta x\) recommendation therefore applies either to the mesh cell size (in non-adaptive runs) or to the effective sub-cell size (in adaptive runs).

Caveats

  • The preloaded VSS pure-species parameters are Bird’s reference values at \(T_{\mathrm{ref}} = 273~\mathrm{K}\). More recent fits exist in the literature for individual species and species pairs; users are encouraged to override the pure-species entries and the pair entries directly in the tables if more appropriate values are available.

  • The calculator assumes a single-temperature gas. Under thermal non-equilibrium (\(T_t \ne T_\mathrm{int}\)) the mean free path depends only on the translational temperature and on the ground-state populations through \(n_s\); the numbers above therefore remain the right first-order estimate provided \(T\) is read as the translational temperature.

  • The Knudsen classification is the standard textbook one (continuum \(< 0.01\), slip \(< 0.1\), transitional \(< 10\), free molecular otherwise); it should be treated as indicative rather than sharp.
