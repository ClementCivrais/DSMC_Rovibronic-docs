Rovibronic level structure

The rovibronic extension replaces the continuous internal energy usually carried on a DSMC particle by an indexed triple \((n, v, J)\) that selects one level out of a three-dimensional energy table. The present section documents how that table is built, from the Born-Oppenheimer separation of electronic and nuclear motion through to the centrifugal-barrier cut-off, and how the associated degeneracies and partition functions are evaluated.

Born-Oppenheimer separation

The total Hamiltonian of a diatomic molecule in the centre-of-mass frame reads

\[\hat{H} = \hat{T}_{N} + \hat{T}_{e} + \hat{V}_{eN} + \hat{V}_{ee} + \hat{V}_{NN},\]

where \(\hat{T}_{N}\) and \(\hat{T}_{e}\) are the kinetic energies of the nuclei and electrons respectively, and the remaining terms collect the Coulombic interactions. Because the electrons are much lighter than the nuclei (\(m_e/m_N \sim 10^{-4}\)), the Born-Oppenheimer approximation separates the problem into a fast electronic sub-problem, solved at fixed nuclear positions and yielding a potential-energy surface (PES) \(V_n(R)\) for each electronic state \(n\), and a slow nuclear sub-problem that evolves on that surface:

\[\Bigl[-\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2\mu}\frac{1}{R^{2}} \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}R} \left(R^{2}\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}R}\right) + \frac{\hbar^{2}\, J(J+1)}{2\mu R^{2}} + V_n(R)\Bigr]\,\chi_{nvJ}(R) = E_{nvJ}\,\chi_{nvJ}(R),\]

where \(\mu\) is the reduced mass, \(\hbar^{2}J(J+1)/(2\mu R^{2})\) is the centrifugal contribution, and \(\chi_{nvJ}(R)\) is the radial vibrational wave-function. The total internal energy is approximated as a sum,

\[E_{\mathrm{int}}(n, v, J) = T_e^{(n)} + E_v^{(n)}(v) + E_r^{(n, v)}(J),\]

where \(T_e^{(n)}\) is the electronic term energy of state \(n\) (the minimum of \(V_n\)), \(E_v^{(n)}\) is the vibrational term in electronic level \(n\), and \(E_r^{(n,v)}\) is the rotational term in the \((n, v)\) manifold.

Vibrational ladder — Morse oscillator

A widely used and tractable model of the molecular PES is the Morse potential

\[V_{\mathrm{M}}(R) = D_e\,\bigl[1 - e^{-a(R - R_e)}\bigr]^{2},\]

with dissociation depth \(D_e\), equilibrium bond length \(R_e\), and range parameter \(a\). The Morse potential has the distinctive feature of admitting an analytical solution of the non-rotating radial Schrödinger equation, whose eigenvalues are

\[E_v^{(n)}(v) = hc\left[\omega_e^{(n)}\left(v + \tfrac{1}{2}\right) - \omega_e x_e^{(n)} \left(v + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)^{2}\right],\]

with the spectroscopic constants \(\omega_e^{(n)}\) (harmonic frequency) and \(\omega_e x_e^{(n)}\) (anharmonicity) related to the Morse parameters through \(\omega_e = a\sqrt{2D_e/\mu}/(2\pi c)\) and \(\omega_e x_e = hc\,\omega_e^{2}/(4 D_e)\). The quadratic correction in \((v + 1/2)\) captures the softening of the vibrational spacing toward dissociation.

The ladder is truncated at the dissociation limit: the maximum bound vibrational quantum number \(v_{\max}^{(n)}\) is determined as the largest \(v\) for which the fitted series still lies below the dissociation energy \(E_d^{(n)}\) of electronic state \(n\). In practice a Dunham series is used in place of the pure Morse expression, which preserves the analytical form of the spacing but allows higher-order terms to be fitted to ab-initio data:

\[E_v^{(n)}(v) = \sum_{k \ge 1} Y_{k,0}^{(n)} \left(v + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)^{k}.\]

Rotational sub-ladder — rigid rotor with distortion

For each \((n, v)\), the rotational term is built on the rigid-rotor energy \(B\,J(J+1)\), augmented by a centrifugal- distortion correction \(-D\,[J(J+1)]^{2}\):

\[E_r^{(n, v)}(J) = B_v\, J(J+1) - D_v\,[J(J+1)]^{2}.\]

The rotational and distortion constants \(B_v\) and \(D_v\) depend on the vibrational state through the rotation-vibration coupling:

\[B_v = B_e - \alpha_e\left(v + \tfrac{1}{2}\right), \qquad D_v = D_e - \beta_e\left(v + \tfrac{1}{2}\right).\]

The maximum rotational quantum number \(J_{\max}^{(n,v)}\) is obtained from a centrifugal-barrier analysis: as \(J\) grows, the effective potential

\[V_{\mathrm{eff}}(R; J) = V_n(R) + \frac{\hbar^{2}\, J(J+1)}{2\mu R^{2}}\]

develops a maximum that eventually falls below the dissociation asymptote; beyond that point the \((n, v, J)\) state is no longer bound. \(J_{\max}^{(n,v)}\) is set to the largest \(J\) for which a bound state persists; the numerical determination loops over \(J\) and tests whether the energy derived from the Dunham expansion is still smaller than the local centrifugal barrier.

Dunham expansion and ab-initio fits

The general expansion carried by the database is

\[E(n, v, J) = T_e^{(n)} + \sum_{k, \ell \ge 0} Y_{k,\ell}^{(n)} \left(v + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)^{k} [J(J+1)]^{\ell},\]

which subsumes the Morse-plus-distortion form as the leading terms. The coefficients \(Y_{k,\ell}^{(n)}\) are fitted, electronic state by electronic state, against ab-initio PECs or against the term values of Huber-Herzberg-style compilations. Fit quality and provenance are documented, species by species, in docs/Database/dunham_coefficients_from_ab_initio_PES.md in the repository.

Degeneracies

The statistical weight of a rovibronic level is the product of rotational and electronic multiplicities:

\[g(n, v, J) = (2J+1)\, g_{\mathrm{elec}}^{(n)},\]

where the \((2J+1)\) factor counts the \(m_J = -J, \ldots, +J\) projections of the rotational angular momentum, and \(g_{\mathrm{elec}}^{(n)}\) is the electronic spatial-spin multiplicity — for instance \(g = 3\) for a \({}^{3}\Sigma\) state, \(g = 2\) for \({}^{2}\Sigma\), and \(g = 4\) for \({}^{2}\Pi\) (spin-orbit doubling included). Nuclear spin contributions to the multiplicity cancel out across reactant and product in the types of balance we are concerned with, and are omitted from the database entries.

Nuclear-spin statistics and ortho/para sub-ladders

For homonuclear diatomics such as N2, O2, and H2, the symmetry of the nuclear wave-function under exchange of the two identical nuclei constrains the allowed \((v, J)\) combinations:

  • symmetric electronic states (\(\Sigma^{+}_{g}\), \(\Pi_{g}\), …) with nuclei of spin \(I\): even \(J\) pair with one total-spin state count, odd \(J\) with the complementary one;

  • antisymmetric electronic states (\(\Sigma^{-}_{u}\), …): the parity is reversed.

The practical consequence is that, for a homonuclear species, only alternate \(J\) values are physically populated within each electronic state. The sampling routines in the solver respect this parity constraint by enforcing the allowed parity at the Larsen-Borgnakke acceptance step, which amounts to halving the effective rotational partition function.

Ground-state normalisation and provenance

Every energy in the database is stored relative to the molecular ground state \((n, v, J) = (0, 0, 0)\):

\[E_{\mathrm{int}}(n, v, J) \leftarrow E_{\mathrm{int}}(n, v, J) - E_{\mathrm{int}}(0, 0, 0),\]

so that the ground state carries zero internal energy by convention. This choice has no physical content, but ensures that the dissociation energy \(D_0\) that the chemistry module reads from the database is the physical bond energy, rather than the potential minimum \(D_e = D_0 + E_v(0, 0, 0)\). It also means that the partition functions defined below are explicitly computed relative to the ground state, which removes the additive constant \(T_e^{(0)}\) from every rate integral.

Partition functions

The internal partition function of species \(s\) at temperature \(T\) is the sum, over all bound rovibronic levels, of the Boltzmann factor weighted by the level degeneracy:

\[Q_s^{\mathrm{int}}(T) = \sum_{n=0}^{N_{\mathrm{el}}^{(s)}-1} \sum_{v=0}^{v_{\max}^{(n)}} \sum_{J=0}^{J_{\max}^{(n,v)}} g(n, v, J)\, \exp\!\left[-\frac{E(n, v, J)}{k_B T}\right].\]

These internal partition functions, together with the translational contribution \(Q_s^{\mathrm{tr}}(T) = V(2\pi m_s k_B T / h^{2})^{3/2}\), enter the equilibrium constants used by the chemistry module (see Neutral chemistry). They are pre-tabulated over the simulation temperature range at solver start-up, which turns the run-time rate-constant lookups into constant-cost bisection calls.

Typical state-space sizes

For a diatomic species in air chemistry, the level space is dense. O2, for example, carries of order six electronic states populated at re-entry temperatures, of the order of 60 vibrational levels per electronic state, and on average some 15 rotational levels per vibrational level, for a total of \(\sim 3 \times 10^{5}\) rovibronic levels. N2 has fewer bound electronic states within the energy range of interest but a denser rotational ladder, and lands at a comparable order of magnitude. The tables are built once, at solver start-up, and cached on disk so that subsequent runs with the same species list incur no rebuild cost.
