Boundary classes

Source: src/lagrangian/DSMC/boundaries/ (basic and derived).

Overview

The boundary subsystem implements the particle-patch interactions that take place whenever a parcel’s trajectory intersects a face that lies on the domain boundary. Boundaries are where the DSMC simulation couples to the outside world — walls, inflows, outflows, periodic surrogates, and any sink through which mass, momentum, and energy leave or enter the domain. Their physical fidelity therefore drives the correctness of every wall-integrated quantity (heat flux, drag, catalytic chemistry) and every inflow-dependent quantity (shock structure, plume profile).

Three families are distinguished by the code base:

  • patch boundaries — walls, inflows, outflows; read from the dsmcPatchBoundaries list in system/boundariesDict;

  • cyclic boundaries — periodic surrogates, reflective particle membranes; read from dsmcCyclicBoundaries;

  • general boundaries — pressure- and mass-flow-driven inlets with a volume component; read from dsmcGeneralBoundaries.

Each entry pairs a patch name with a boundaryModel drawn from the classes listed below. The associated configuration lives in an adjacent <BoundaryModel>Properties sub-dictionary.

Common wall physics

All wall-patch classes share the same underlying kinematic problem. When a parcel of mass \(m_s\) reaches a wall face with pre-collision velocity \(\mathbf{v}\), the wall is characterised by its local (possibly patch-varying) velocity \(\mathbf{v}_w\), temperature \(T_w\), and inward normal \(\hat{\mathbf{n}}_w\). The reflected velocity \(\mathbf{v}'\) is drawn from one of several scattering kernels (specular, diffuse, mixed, CLL, rovibronic-aware). Three quantities are accumulated per face on every interaction — momentum flux, kinetic-energy flux, and internal-energy flux — by the boundaryMeasurements subsystem (see Measurements), which is the authoritative source of surface heat flux and drag.

The key physical ingredients across wall-type classes are:

  • Thermal accommodation coefficient \(\alpha_T\), the fraction of the kinetic-energy difference transferred to the wall:

    \[\alpha_T = \frac{E_i - E_r}{E_i - E_w},\]

    where \(E_i\) and \(E_r\) are the incident and reflected kinetic energies per molecule, and \(E_w\) is the kinetic energy a molecule would have if fully equilibrated with the wall. \(\alpha_T = 0\) corresponds to specular reflection (no energy exchange); \(\alpha_T = 1\) to full thermal accommodation.

  • Momentum accommodation coefficient \(\sigma\), the fraction of the tangential momentum transferred to the wall. The Maxwell mixed model uses a single scalar \(\sigma = \alpha\); the Cercignani-Lampis-Lord (CLL) model separates the normal and tangential components.

  • Internal-energy accommodation — whether and how the reflected parcel’s internal state is re-sampled from the wall temperature. The rovibronic-aware wall (dsmcMixedDiffuseSpecularWallRotPatch) is unique in sampling on the discrete rovibronic state space; all other wall classes leave the internal state unchanged or sample the continuous-variable equivalent.

Wall scattering kernels in detail

Specular reflection

The simplest kernel: the velocity component normal to the patch face is reversed, the tangential components are unchanged,

\[\mathbf{v}' = \mathbf{v} - 2\,(\mathbf{v}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}})\,\hat{\mathbf{n}},\]

and the internal state of the parcel is unaffected. No momentum, energy, or mass is exchanged with the wall. Appropriate for:

  • symmetry planes (the reflection is exact by construction);

  • free-molecular verification tests, where one wants to isolate the inflow kernel;

  • periodic flow surrogates on a channel’s solid wall when the physics is wall-independent.

Fully diffuse reflection

The reflected velocity is drawn from a half-range Maxwellian at the wall temperature \(T_w\), drifted by the wall velocity \(\mathbf{v}_w\):

\[f_w(\mathbf{v}) \propto (\mathbf{v}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}_w)\; \exp\!\left[-\frac{m\,|\mathbf{v} - \mathbf{v}_w|^{2}} {2 k_B T_w}\right], \qquad \mathbf{v}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}_w > 0.\]

Sampling proceeds as follows:

  1. draw the normal component \(v_n > 0\) from the inverse of the half-range CDF;

  2. draw the two tangential components from Maxwell-Boltzmann at \(T_w\), independently;

  3. rotate the resulting velocity into the face-local frame and add \(\mathbf{v}_w\).

The kernel corresponds to full thermal and tangential-momentum accommodation (\(\alpha_T = \sigma = 1\)) and is the textbook “cold wall” boundary condition for re-entry simulations. On the internal side, the continuous-internal- energy implementation redraws the parcel’s internal energy from a Boltzmann distribution at \(T_w\) via the standard Larsen-Borgnakke acceptance step; the rovibronic-aware variant (below) does the same on the discrete state space.

Maxwell mixed model

Defined by a single accommodation coefficient \(\alpha \in [0, 1]\) (diffuseFraction): a fraction \(\alpha\) of incoming parcels reflect diffusely, the remainder specularly. Simple, but does not reproduce the smoothly interpolated scattering pattern that experiments report for most technical surfaces.

Cercignani-Lampis-Lord (CLL)

The CLL kernel [Lord1991b] provides two independent accommodation coefficients, \(\alpha_n\) for the normal component and \(\alpha_t\) for the tangential component, and a smooth interpolation between specular and fully diffuse. In scaled variables \(V_n, V_t = v_n, v_t / \sqrt{2 k_B T_w / m}\), the post-collision velocities are sampled from

\[V_n' = \sqrt{(1 - \alpha_n)\,V_n^{2} + 2\,\sqrt{(1 - \alpha_n)\,\alpha_n}\,V_n\,V_r \cos\theta + \alpha_n\,V_r^{2}},\]
\[V_t' = (1 - \alpha_t)\,V_t + \sqrt{\alpha_t\,(2 - \alpha_t)}\,V_r',\]

with \(V_r, V_r'\) Maxwell-Boltzmann-distributed thermal magnitudes and \(\theta\) uniform on \([0, 2\pi)\). The two limits

  • \(\alpha_n = \alpha_t = 0\) — specular;

  • \(\alpha_n = \alpha_t = 1\) — fully diffuse;

are recovered from the same kernel.

The CLL model respects reciprocity (forward / reverse detailed balance) at any \((\alpha_n, \alpha_t)\), unlike the Maxwell mixed model in which the mixture breaks reciprocity for inhomogeneous accommodation. For this reason, CLL is preferred when quantitative drag and heat-flux predictions on realistic surfaces are the target, and a measured \((\alpha_n, \alpha_t)\) for the relevant surface is available.

Rovibronic-aware wall — dsmcMixedDiffuseSpecularWallRotPatch

This is the only boundary class that samples on the quantum-state space at reflection. The velocity kernel is the Maxwell mixed model; on a diffuse reflection the internal state \((n, v, J)\) is redrawn from the Boltzmann distribution over the species’ rovibronic levels at \(T_w\),

\[P(n, v, J) = \frac{g(n, v, J)\,\exp\!\bigl[-E(n, v, J)/k_B T_w\bigr]} {\displaystyle \sum_{n', v', J'} g(n', v', J')\,\exp\!\bigl[-E(n', v', J')/k_B T_w\bigr]}.\]

The partition function in the denominator is the full internal partition function documented in Rovibronic level structure. Two consequences of note:

  • under a wall temperature \(T_w\) small compared to the lowest electronic term \(T_e^{(1)}\), the wall leaves every reflected parcel in the ground electronic state, which is the usual physical expectation for a cold catalytic wall;

  • when the bulk gas is hotter than the wall (re-entry), the wall progressively cools the reflected internal distribution and feeds an inward-directed internal-energy flux, which the boundaryMeasurements subsystem records as internalEBF_.

This is the right choice for every simulation in which the internal populations impinging on the wall must be equilibrated to the wall itself — radiation coupling, state-resolved catalysis, non-equilibrium boundary layers, or simply more faithful drag predictions on hot-wall surfaces.

Inflow models

Free-stream Maxwellian inflow

The dsmcFreeStreamInflowPatch injects parcels drawn from a Maxwellian distribution at prescribed \(n_\infty, T_\infty, \mathbf{v}_\infty\). The expected number of parcels entering the domain through a face of area \(A_f\) during \(\Delta t\) follows from the half-range flux integral,

\[\dot{N}_{\mathrm{in}} = \frac{n_\infty\,A_f\,\Delta t} {\sqrt{2\pi\,m/k_B T_\infty}}\, \bigl[\exp(-s^{2}) + \sqrt{\pi}\,s\,(1 + \mathrm{erf}\,s)\bigr],\]

with \(s = (\mathbf{v}_\infty\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}_w) / \sqrt{2 k_B T_\infty/m}\) the speed ratio. Parcel velocities are then drawn from the half-range Maxwellian via Bird’s acceptance-rejection algorithm. The internal state is sampled from the Boltzmann distribution at \(T_\infty\) on the rovibronic level space. Sub-dictionary:

dsmcFreeStreamInflowPatchProperties
{
    typeIds         (N2 O2);
    velocity        (7000 0 0);
    temperature     300;
    numberDensities
    {
        N2    5.6e22;
        O2    1.5e22;
    };
}

Chapman-Enskog first-order inflow

dsmcChapmanEnskogFreeStreamInflowPatch extends the Maxwellian inflow with a Chapman-Enskog first-order correction,

\[f(\mathbf{v}) = f_{\mathrm{MB}}(\mathbf{v})\, \Bigl[1 + \tfrac{2}{5}\,\tilde{\mathbf{q}}\cdot\mathbf{c} \,(|\mathbf{c}|^{2} - 5/2) + \tilde{\boldsymbol{\tau}} : \mathbf{c}\,\mathbf{c} \Bigr],\]

with \(\mathbf{c}\) the peculiar velocity in units of the most-probable speed, and \(\tilde{\mathbf{q}}, \tilde{\boldsymbol{\tau}}\) the scaled heat-flux and stress tensors. This is the natural inflow when coupling DSMC to a continuum upstream: the first-order moments \(\tilde{\mathbf{q}},\tilde{\boldsymbol{\tau}}\) are passed from the Navier-Stokes solution, and the DSMC inflow reproduces them at the patch. Without this correction the inflow Maxwellian is inconsistent with any non-zero upstream stress, which biases the early part of the DSMC domain.

Pressure-driven inlets

dsmcWangPressureInlet, dsmcLiouFangPressureInlet, and dsmcMassFlowRateInlet apply Maxwellian sampling at an inferred density chosen so that the desired pressure (\(p_0\)) or mass-flow rate (\(\dot{m}\)) is respected in the long-time average. They are the natural choice when the upstream reservoir is characterised through \(p_0\) or \(\dot{m}\) rather than through \(n_\infty\) — typically vacuum-chamber and nozzle plume studies.

Each of the three implements a slightly different control law; the Wang variant updates the inflow number density on the fly from the observed patch-average pressure, the Liou-Fang variant uses an upstream-stagnation-based estimator, and the mass-flow-rate variant enforces \(\dot{m}\) directly. Pick the one that matches the form in which the upstream condition is specified.

Particle-deletion patch

dsmcDeletionPatch removes any parcel that reaches the patch. The sub-dictionary supports either the allSpecies switch or a typeIds list for species-selective outflow:

dsmcDeletionPatchProperties
{
    allSpecies      yes;
    // typeIds      (N2 O2);   // exclusive alternative
}

In high-altitude free-stream simulations with closed-box geometry (e.g. a plume emanating from a small inlet into a large background), the deletion patch is the most efficient way to cap the domain; any parcel that has left the region of interest is no longer worth tracking.

Cyclic boundaries

The dsmcCyclicBoundaries list accepts pairings of two patches that are treated as periodically connected: a parcel that crosses one face re-enters at the paired face with an offset position. The dsmcReflectiveParticleMembranePatch variant reflects a user-specified fraction of incoming parcels back into the domain while letting the rest pass through, which is the mechanism used, for instance, to model permeable membranes in micro/nano-flow applications.

General boundaries

The dsmcGeneralBoundaries list accepts non-patch, non-cyclic boundary types that operate on a patch surface and also carry a volume component — the pressure-driven inlets and the Chapman-Enskog inflow among them. Their configuration follows the same generalBoundaryProperties { patchName … } / <model>Properties { … } two-block structure as the patch boundaries.

Boundary measurements

Independently of which patch class is attached, a boundaryMeasurements object accumulates per-patch, per-face diagnostics on every particle impact (see Measurements for the catalogue of accumulated quantities). These are the ingredients from which every wall-level analysis — surface heat flux, drag, friction, internal-energy deposition — is constructed, and they are available at the end of a run through the standard OpenFOAM boundary-field output. The rovibronic-aware internalEBF_ accumulator is the key addition over stock dsmcFoam+: it carries the state-resolved internal energy delivered to the wall, and is therefore the correct input to any surface model (catalysis, heat-shield ablation) that depends on the detailed internal state of the impinging molecules.

Choosing a wall model

A pragmatic decision tree:

  • Symmetry plane, free-molecular test, non-dissipative surface → dsmcSpecularWallPatch.

  • Cold thermally-accommodated wall, no surface chemistry → dsmcDiffuseWallPatch.

  • Realistic technical surface with measured :math:`alpha_n, alpha_t` → dsmcCLLWallPatch.

  • Simulation coupled to radiation or surface catalysis, or any case where the internal populations at the wall matter → dsmcMixedDiffuseSpecularWallRotPatch.

  • Spatially varying (e.g. heated strip on a cold plate) → the …FieldPatch variant of any of the above.

For inflow:

  • Free-stream at prescribed Mach and altitude → dsmcFreeStreamInflowPatch.

  • Coupled-domain interface with continuum upstream → dsmcChapmanEnskogFreeStreamInflowPatch.

  • Vacuum chamber / reservoir, characterised by pressure or mass-flow rate → one of the three pressure-inlet classes.

[Lord1991b]

R. G. Lord, “Some extensions to the Cercignani-Lampis gas-surface scattering kernel,” Physics of Fluids A, 3 (4), 706–710 (1991).
